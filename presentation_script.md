## Slide 1 -- Title

> Good morning/afternoon. My name is Yvo Hu, and this is my MSc thesis: *Measuring Placement-Sensitive Resilience under Chaos -- a pre-registered, layered study in Kubernetes.* The tool I built is ChaosProbe.
>
> The whole talk fits on three lines. A single resilience score is blind to where you place your pods. Placement does move a real kernel-level mechanism -- but that mechanism does not reach the user. And where placement *does* reach users is availability under node failure, which turns out to be predictable from the dependency graph.

---

## Slide 2 -- The Operator's Decision

> Consider a concrete decision. An operator runs a ten-service microservice storefront on a small Kubernetes cluster and must choose: pack the services onto a few nodes, or spread them across many?
>
> Two literatures give opposite advice. The locality literature says pack -- co-located services keep their calls node-local, and network-aware schedulers report response-time reductions up to 37 percent. The availability literature says spread -- services in distinct failure domains survive the loss of any one node -- and the interference literature warns that co-location invites contention.
>
> What does today's tooling tell this operator? The Kubernetes scheduler scores nodes on resource fit, not fault isolation -- it answers a different question. Chaos-engineering tools answer the resilience question directly, but with a single aggregate number. And whether that number can even *discriminate* between placements -- whether its between-condition signal exceeds run-to-run noise -- is, to our knowledge, unexamined.
>
> That is the gap. These two bodies of practice have never been connected. Nobody has measured *at which layer* a placement effect appears under a given fault class, whether it reaches the user, and whether an aggregate score can see it at all.

---

## Slide 3 -- Research Question

> The research question: *under which chaos fault classes does pod placement measurably affect mechanism-level behaviour and user-visible outcomes -- and when do aggregate resilience scores obscure those effects?*
>
> This is framed as a fault-class-by-measurement-layer study, not a placement ranking and not a refutation of the literature. An effect can appear at three layers -- the aggregate score, a kernel/network mechanism, or the user-visible outcome -- and these layers need not agree.
>
> Placement is manipulated by two orthogonal knobs: the cross-node dependency-edge fraction *f*, from packing at zero to spreading at one; and the replication degree *r*, packed versus anti-affine. Plus two interventional arms -- a NodeLocal DNSCache toggle, which we ran, and the kube-proxy mode, which we de-scoped to ipvs.
>
> One framing point: reliability comes first. If a single score could rank placements reliably, the layered design would just be a refinement. Because its availability layer cannot in this regime, the layered design is what produces the findings at all.

---

## Slide 4 -- Pre-Registration & Provenance

> This slide is the methodological backbone, and it is the first contribution. To answer the question without the freedom to choose tests after seeing the data, the study is pre-registered.
>
> The hypotheses, the smallest effect sizes of interest, the per-cell sample sizes, and the analysis *code* were frozen before any confirmatory data was collected. Each hypothesis carries a registered effect-size or reliability bar -- so statistical significance alone is never enough.
>
> On the provenance side: independent single-commit sessions are the unit of analysis, every session is gated by `doctor --strict` -- clean tree, scenario hash present, git not dirty -- and each campaign's raw data is committed and hash-stamped before its analysis is written. Every number in the thesis traces to an archived, hash-stamped run.
>
> The payoff is honesty: this converts "we observed placement effects" into "here is exactly which effects survive a strict bar, and which do not."

## Slide 5 -- ChaosProbe, the Framework

> ChaosProbe itself. The authored parts: a placement solver that drives the two knobs by deployment-level mutation; cross-layer probers -- Prometheus mechanism metrics, route latency with a dependent-versus-control confound split, and EndpointSlice availability snapshots; the cross-node fraction computed from a Neo4j dependency graph plus the realized placement; and a layered scorecard with separate availability, mechanism, and user-tail sub-scores instead of one number.
>
> Underneath, off-the-shelf infrastructure executes the science but does not define it: LitmusChaos runs the faults, Prometheus and Locust collect and load, Neo4j stores the graph, and Online Boutique is the system under test.
>
> The line between the two matters for a defense: the science -- what to vary, what to measure, what to believe -- is mine; the third-party components carry it out.

---

## Slide 6 -- Three-Layer Measurement Design

> Every experiment is a three-phase window: 60 seconds of steady state, 120 seconds of active fault, 60 seconds of recovery -- read at every layer by six continuous probers running as background threads.
>
> The aggregate score is the layered scorecard. The mechanism layer is the UDP-conntrack drop, kube-proxy SLO, CoreDNS latency -- the SIG-Scalability metrics. The user layer is HTTP route p95 and error rate, split into dependent and control routes. And under node-drain, availability is read directly from EndpointSlice troughs -- the fraction of app-ready endpoints lost.
>
> Why three layers and not one score? Because a single number's failure modes compound. Under churn the score is noisy -- the availability layer it leans on has little reproducible signal. Under node-drain it is *absent* -- the drain takes down the infrastructure the probes depend on, every probe returns Unknown, and the score is undefined exactly when the availability outcome is most dramatic. So we read availability from EndpointSlice troughs instead.

## Slide 7 -- Campaigns & Hypotheses

> The evidence is three pre-registered campaigns, each frozen before analysis. C1: eight complete-block pod-delete dose sessions sweeping *f* -- tests H1, H4, H5. C2: 24 node-drain sessions crossing replication and mode -- tests H3. C3: 14 pod-delete sessions toggling the DNS cache -- tests H2.
>
> Two fault classes run through everything: churn, where pod-delete removes a single replica; and node failure, where draining a node has a placement-dependent blast radius.
>
> The hypotheses, stated bar-first. H1: a dose-response of the east-west tail, monotone and at least 15 percent. H2: placement-dependence plus a DNS intervention, both must pass. H3: replication rescues availability under drain, but only when spread. H4: a descriptive placement frontier. H5: the layered scorecard is more reliable than a single aggregate, both layers at ICC 0.5 or better. H6 -- direction transfer in iptables mode -- was not attempted, because the cluster ran ipvs.

---

## Slide 8 -- H1: Dose-Response of the East-West Tail

> Registered bar: median east-west p95 rises monotonically in *f*, by at least 15 percent from packed to spread.
>
> The result: Page's L trend test gives L = 410, z = 3.54, p = 0.0002 -- a real, Holm-significant monotone trend. But the total effect from *f*=0 to *f*=1 is only plus 13.35 percent, below the registered 15 percent SESOI, and the medians rise then dip slightly at *f*=0.75. So H1 is *not supported*: the dose-response is real and statistically significant, but practically negligible at the registered bar.
>
> One disclosure. This analysis carries the study's single outcome-aware deviation, D3. The registered pre-window taint flags every iteration at *f*=0.25 and 0.5, leaving zero complete blocks, which makes Page's L uncomputable -- H1 would be unrunnable as registered. We withdraw the taint on protocol-independent grounds: it gates the DNS/UDP pool, a different protocol from the TCP/gRPC east-west outcome. This is the one deviation a reader may weigh differently, and it is logged in full.

---

## Slide 9 -- H2: Placement-Dependence and the DNS Intervention

> H2 is a both-must-pass conjunction on the during-churn UDP-conntrack drop. Arm (a): spread should show a larger drop than packed. Arm (b): the NodeLocal DNSCache should shrink it by at least 50 percent.
>
> Arm (b) passes strongly -- a 78 percent median shrinkage, every pair between 61 and 85 percent. But arm (a) *reverses*: packed exceeds spread in all 7 of 7 cache-off pairs. So the conjunction is false and H2 is not supported.
>
> The reversal is the interesting part. Co-locating a service's replicas on one node concentrates the per-node conntrack churn when pod-delete hits that node -- a larger drop than spreading the same replicas. And the DNS cache removes the drop under *both* placements, which tells us the floodable pool is the general per-node DNS/UDP churn, not specifically cross-node DNS. So placement does matter for conntrack -- significantly -- but with the opposite sign to the prediction. And the DNS cache is the one actionable lever the study confirmed.

---

## Slide 10 -- H3: Replication Rescue under Node-Drain

> H3 predicts an interaction: replication rescues availability under node-drain only when replicas don't share a failure domain. Two co-primary outcomes -- trough depth and user-route error -- both must pass.
>
> Split verdict. On the user-route error face, anti-affine r=3 fully rescues: the error rate goes from 0.632 to zero, a significant interaction, clearing the 0.302 margin comfortably. On the trough-depth face, the interaction is significant -- p = 0.0065 -- but the rescue can't clear its margin, and here's why: under round-robin spread, draining one node costs r=1 only about one pod, so the r=1 depth of 0.091 *equals* the one-pod margin it has to beat. The metric's dynamic range coincides with the bar -- there's no room for a rescue to register.
>
> So the conjunction fails, but it fails on a criterion the depth face could not adjudicate by construction. We report it exactly as registered, not retuned -- and the design-fix slide shows the rescue is real once that artifact is removed.

---

## Slide 11 -- H4 and H5: Frontier and Scorecard Reliability

> Two availability-side results, and both reveal the same thing: the availability axis is dead under pod-delete.
>
> H4 is the descriptive frontier -- latency face against availability face. All five placements come out non-dominated, because the availability face is degenerate: pod-delete removes a single pod, so trough depth is about one pod for every placement. The latency face varies -- that's the H1 signal -- but a single-face advantage can't establish dominance. The trade-off the frontier was designed to reveal is simply not realizable from this data.
>
> H5 is the scorecard reliability test. The required conjunction fails: the availability sub-score's ICC is 0.180, below the 0.5 bar. But look at the mechanism layer -- ICC 0.994, versus a naive single aggregate's 0.066. That is the headline reliability result: the layered scorecard's mechanism sub-score is far more test-retest reliable than a single number. The availability failure is a sensible falsification -- pod-delete produces no sustained outage, so there's little reproducible availability signal to estimate.
>
> (If asked: the 0.994 mechanism ICC is computed with the D3 taint withdrawn, like H1 -- but unlike H1, H5's FAIL verdict is robust to that choice; it fails the conjunction either way.)

## Slide 12 -- The Confirmatory Family: Holm Capstone

> The four confirmatory hypotheses, corrected together by Holm. (Note the family is four, not six: H4 is descriptive with no p-value, and H6 was de-scoped -- both sit outside the correction.) H1's trend and H3's interaction both survive Holm as significant. But Holm-significance is necessary, not sufficient -- each member also carries a registered effect-size or reliability bar the p-value can't speak to, and every member fails its bar or its significance.
>
> Family verdict: no confirmatory hypothesis is supported.
>
> But read the positive thread. The mechanism -- the UDP-conntrack reconvergence signature -- is real and individually significant *every* time it is measured: placement-dependent under churn in H2, interacting with replication under drain in H3's error face, and the one scorecard layer with near-perfect reliability in H5. What does not hold, at the pre-registered bar, is that this mechanism translates into the user-visible advantages the hypotheses predicted. Once pod-delete at r=1 is understood as a pure churn fault, that bound is the expected shape: during the kill window the single replica is just gone, and every dependent request fails identically whether services are packed or spread. The contribution is a real mechanism, rigorously bounded in reach.

## Slide 13 -- Design-Corrected Re-Analysis (Exploratory)

> Three of the availability-side results were construction-limited for one reason: pod-delete at r=1 removes a service's only replica, so the availability trough is about one pod for every placement and the axis cannot move. That made H3's depth un-passable, H4's frontier degenerate, and H5's availability ICC a measurement of noise.
>
> So, as an exploratory follow-up outside the frozen family -- with criteria pre-declared before the new data were examined -- we recompute under node-drain, whose blast radius varies with placement. New campaign C4, a node-drain dose-response.
>
> The axis is now live: the availability trough goes from 1.0 when packed to 0.36 when spread, and a genuine latency-versus-availability trade-off appears -- spreading buys a large availability gain at a small latency cost. And re-analysing the C2 node-drain data, anti-affine replication drives the user error to zero with a significant interaction; the depth also halves, but that threshold is knife-edge at n=8, so the robust evidence is the interaction and the error elimination, not the depth number. One honesty note on the corrected availability ICC: under node-drain it is 1.0, but that is 1.0 *by construction* -- placement is deterministic so between-session variance is identically zero. It means large, well-defined signal, not a high test-retest estimate.
>
> The honest scope: this does *not* re-open the frozen verdicts. It shows that H3's not-supported and H4's degenerate results were partly construction artifacts whose underlying effects are real once availability can move.

## Slide 14 -- External Validity: A Second Workload

> The single largest threat is that all of this comes from one workload. So as an exploratory check -- outside the Holm family -- we replicated the two placement-bearing studies on a deliberately different application: DeathStarBench hotelReservation, a deep gRPC fan-out over Consul service discovery, against Online Boutique's flatter topology.
>
> Both arms corroborate. H1 shows *no* dose-response -- Page's L p = 0.99, a tight 5-to-9 millisecond band, if anything trending down. H3 reaches the same conjunction-false verdict by the same route: a significant interaction, anti-affine directionally best. One precision point: hotelReservation actually fails *more broadly* than online-boutique -- here *neither* margin is cleared (depth 0.044, error 0.212), whereas online-boutique cleared the user-error margin decisively. So the second workload reproduces the conjunction-false *verdict*, not online-boutique's clean error-face rescue.
>
> The cross-workload picture mirrors the within-workload one: the mechanism -- spreading shrinks the blast radius and speeds recovery -- is robust on both applications; the strong placement and margin-clearing claims hold on neither. That a second, structurally different workload reproduces both negatives is the strongest available evidence the central finding is not an artifact of one topology.

## Slide 15 -- Threats to Validity

> The boundary of the claims, stated plainly. The single-replica baseline makes pod-delete a pure churn fault -- production runs multiple replicas, and the design-fix addresses the availability axis. One workload -- partly addressed by hotelReservation. A virtualized cluster, where bare-metal I/O may differ, and conntrack behaviour is pinned to Kubernetes v1.28.6. IPVS only -- the iptables direction-transfer comparison was de-scoped. And the one non-blind deviation, D3, disclosed with a sensitivity check.
>
> Against those, what protects the claims: pre-registration fixing the SESOIs against a measured noise floor before collection; Holm correction; the `doctor --strict` gate and discard-not-patch rule; pre-registration frozen before analysis; the dependent-versus-control route split controlling the user layer; and a second workload corroborating the central finding.

## Slide 16 -- Conclusion & Future Work

> Three contributions. First, the pre-registered, provenance-gated measurement methodology -- reusable and independent of the empirical outcome. Second, a positively-established, precisely-bounded mechanism: a real conntrack signature that the DNS cache removes and that does not reach the user at the registered bar. Third, a reproducible campaign protocol with hash-stamped, pre-registered artifacts.
>
> The practical findings: don't trust a single aggregate score to rank placements -- audit its reliability first; measure the layer your fault class perturbs; placement advice is fault-class advice, and even its sign can invert; and the DNS-cache intervention is the one concrete, placement-independent mitigation.
>
> Future work: replicate the family on different infrastructure -- a managed cluster, a different proxy mode, bare metal; design a rescue margin the depth face can actually express; apportion the conntrack drop between kernel TCP teardown and kube-proxy's UDP cleanup; and integrate the cross-node fraction into a scheduler as a scoring plugin.
>
> The one-line answer: a single score is blind to placement; placement acts at the mechanism layer without reaching the user; and where it does reach users is availability under node failure -- predictably, from the dependency graph. A real mechanism, rigorously bounded in reach.
